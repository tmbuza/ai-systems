Evaluation and Quality Control

Published

Jun 2026

  • ID: AI-006
  • Type: Systems Guide
  • Audience: AI users, analysts, educators, researchers, managers, and workflow designers
  • Theme: AI outputs must be evaluated before they are trusted or used

AI systems are not complete when an answer is generated.

They are complete only when the output has been reviewed, tested, documented, and placed in the correct human decision context.

This is one of the most important differences between casual AI use and AI systems work.

Casual AI use often stops at the first useful-looking answer.

AI systems work asks a different question:

Can this output be trusted for this purpose?

That question requires evaluation.

Why Evaluation Matters

AI tools can produce fluent answers that sound confident even when they are incomplete, unsupported, outdated, biased, or wrong.

This creates a serious workflow risk.

The problem is not only that AI can make mistakes.

Humans can also make mistakes.

The deeper problem is that AI output can look polished before it has been verified.

In CDI-style workflows, evaluation is not optional. It is a required layer between AI assistance and human action.

Human defines the task
        ↓
AI generates or assists
        ↓
Human evaluates the output
        ↓
Human revises, documents, decides, or acts

The evaluation step protects the quality of the final decision.

Evaluation Is Context-Specific

There is no single universal score that tells us whether an AI output is good.

A useful output depends on the task.

For example, a good brainstorming answer may be:

  • broad,
  • creative,
  • exploratory,
  • and open-ended.

A good clinical, legal, financial, or scientific answer must be:

  • grounded,
  • cautious,
  • traceable,
  • current,
  • and reviewed by appropriate human expertise.

The same AI model can be useful in both settings, but the evaluation standard must change.

That is why every AI workflow should define quality criteria before relying on output.

Common Evaluation Dimensions

A practical AI evaluation workflow can begin with several dimensions.

Dimension Evaluation question
Accuracy Is the output factually correct for the task?
Completeness Does it answer all important parts of the request?
Relevance Does it stay focused on the user’s actual problem?
Grounding Is the output supported by provided data, sources, or context?
Clarity Is the output understandable to the intended audience?
Safety Could the output cause harm if used directly?
Bias Does the output make unfair, unsupported, or stereotyping assumptions?
Reproducibility Can the workflow be repeated or audited later?
Actionability Does the output support a clear next step?

These dimensions do not all have equal importance in every workflow.

A writing assistant may emphasize clarity and tone.

A data workflow may emphasize reproducibility, grounding, and traceability.

A high-stakes decision workflow may emphasize safety, expert review, and documented limitations.

A Simple AI Output Review Checklist

Before using an AI-generated output, ask:

  1. What task was the AI asked to perform?
  2. What information did the AI use?
  3. What information was missing?
  4. What claims need verification?
  5. What parts are useful as-is?
  6. What parts require editing or correction?
  7. What risks would appear if this output were used directly?
  8. Who needs to review this before action?
  9. What should be documented?
  10. What should not be trusted without additional evidence?

This checklist turns evaluation into a habit.

It also reinforces the Human–AI–Human workflow.

AI assists, but humans remain responsible for judgment, verification, and action.

Task Risk and Evaluation Depth

Not every AI task requires the same level of review.

Low-risk tasks may need light review.

Medium-risk tasks need structured review.

High-risk tasks need expert review, source verification, documentation, and clear accountability.

Task risk Example Evaluation depth
Low Rewriting a non-sensitive paragraph Check tone, clarity, and meaning
Medium Summarizing meeting notes or project plans Check completeness, omissions, and action items
High Medical, legal, financial, employment, or public claims Verify sources, involve expertise, document uncertainty
System-level Automated workflows or repeated AI outputs Evaluate outputs over time and monitor failure patterns

The riskier the task, the more important the evaluation layer becomes.

Evaluation as a Workflow, Not a Feeling

A common mistake is to judge AI output based on whether it “looks good.”

That is not enough.

A better approach is to make evaluation visible.

For example, an AI task log can record:

  • the task,
  • the prompt,
  • the context used,
  • the model or tool used,
  • the output type,
  • the human reviewer,
  • quality scores,
  • revision notes,
  • and final decision status.

This turns AI use into a documented workflow.

That documentation is especially important when AI becomes part of repeated work.

Example AI Task Log

The scaffolded guide includes a small example dataset for AI task evaluation.

The file is created by:

bash scripts/bash/01-create-example-ai-task-log.sh

The expected output is:

data/example/ai-task-log.csv

A simplified version of the task log may include columns like:

task_id,task_type,risk_level,prompt_version,output_reviewed,accuracy_score,clarity_score,grounding_score,human_decision

This does not evaluate the AI model in a laboratory sense.

Instead, it evaluates the usefulness and reliability of AI outputs inside a human workflow.

That is the level of evaluation most applied AI users need first.

Supporting Script: Evaluate AI Output Quality

The scaffold includes a Python script for summarizing evaluation scores.

Run it with:

python scripts/python/02-evaluate-ai-output-quality.py \
  data/example/ai-task-log.csv \
  data/results

Expected output:

data/results/ai-evaluation-summary.csv

This script is intentionally simple.

It demonstrates how a project can move from informal AI review into a structured quality-control table.

In a larger AI system, this same idea can be extended to include:

  • reviewer agreement,
  • task-level pass/fail criteria,
  • source checks,
  • hallucination flags,
  • fairness checks,
  • and revision history.

Supporting Script: Plot Evaluation Summary

The scaffold also includes an R script for creating a visual summary.

Run it with:

Rscript scripts/R/04-plot-evaluation-summary.R \
  data/results/ai-evaluation-summary.csv \
  data/reports

Expected output:

data/reports/ai-evaluation-summary.png

The plot is not meant to prove that an AI system is safe or correct.

It is meant to make review patterns visible.

Visibility is the beginning of quality control.

Evaluation Failure Modes

AI evaluation can fail in predictable ways.

Failure mode What happens
Fluency bias A polished answer is mistaken for a correct answer
Confirmation bias The reviewer accepts outputs that match prior beliefs
Missing context The AI responds without important background information
Weak grounding The output makes claims that are not supported by sources or data
Unclear task criteria Nobody defined what a good output should look like
No review record The final decision cannot be audited later
Automation drift A workflow works once but fails when repeated in new contexts

The goal of evaluation is not perfection.

The goal is controlled, visible, accountable improvement.

Human Review Is Not a Formality

Human review is not just a final glance.

It is an active decision step.

A reviewer should be able to say:

  • what was accepted,
  • what was changed,
  • what was rejected,
  • what remains uncertain,
  • and why the final output is suitable for its intended use.

This is especially important when AI output is used in reports, public communication, education, research, business decisions, or health-related contexts.

Human review is where responsibility re-enters the workflow.

Practical Evaluation Template

A reusable AI output evaluation note can look like this:

Task:

AI tool or model used:

Input context provided:

Output reviewed by:

Accuracy notes:

Grounding notes:

Clarity notes:

Safety or risk concerns:

Required revisions:

Final decision:

Limitations:

This can be used as a lightweight review record.

For higher-stakes work, the template should be expanded with source links, reviewer names, version numbers, and approval status.

CDI Principle

A CDI AI workflow should not ask only:

Did the AI produce an answer?

It should ask:

Was the answer evaluated well enough for the decision it supports?

This principle keeps AI work connected to data, insight, and impact.

Chapter Summary

In this chapter, we introduced AI evaluation and quality control as essential parts of AI systems work.

We distinguished casual output acceptance from structured review.

We defined common evaluation dimensions such as accuracy, completeness, grounding, clarity, safety, bias, reproducibility, and actionability.

We also introduced simple supporting scripts for summarizing and visualizing AI task evaluation records.

Most importantly, we reinforced the Human–AI–Human workflow:

Human defines the task.
AI assists.
Human evaluates, decides, documents, and acts.

Evaluation is the bridge between AI output and responsible use.
