Problem Framing for AI

Published

Jun 2026

  • ID: AI-SYS-03
  • Type: Core Chapter
  • Audience: Learners, analysts, educators, knowledge workers, technical teams, and decision-makers using AI in real workflows
  • Theme: Effective AI work begins with a well-framed human problem

AI systems do not begin with prompts.

They begin with problems.

Before a model is asked to summarize, generate, classify, analyze, automate, or recommend, a human must decide what problem is being addressed, why it matters, who will use the result, and how success will be judged.

This chapter focuses on problem framing for AI.

Problem framing is the step where a broad need is converted into a clear, bounded, responsible, and evaluable AI task.

It is where the Human–AI–Human workflow begins.

Human frames the problem
        ↓
AI assists with generation, reasoning, analysis, or automation
        ↓
Human evaluates, decides, documents, and acts

If the first human step is weak, the rest of the workflow becomes unstable.

A vague problem produces vague prompts.

A vague prompt produces unreliable outputs.

Unreliable outputs create confusion, rework, or risk.

The goal of this chapter is to help you slow down before using AI and define the work clearly enough that AI assistance becomes useful, reviewable, and responsible.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • distinguish a broad goal from an AI-ready task;
  • define the user, purpose, input, output, and decision context for an AI workflow;
  • identify whether a task is appropriate for AI assistance;
  • classify AI tasks by risk, complexity, and required human review;
  • convert an unclear AI request into a structured problem statement;
  • prepare a problem-framing template that can be reused across CDI AI workflows.

Why Problem Framing Matters

Many AI failures are not model failures.

They are framing failures.

The user asks the wrong question.

The context is incomplete.

The expected output is unclear.

The audience is not defined.

The risk level is underestimated.

The result is used for a decision it was not designed to support.

AI tools can produce fluent answers even when the task is poorly framed. That fluency can hide uncertainty, missing context, or weak assumptions.

Problem framing protects the workflow by making the human intention explicit before AI enters the process.

In CDI terms, problem framing turns AI use from an isolated interaction into part of a system.

From Broad Goal to AI-Ready Task

A broad goal is usually too large for direct AI use.

For example:

Help me improve this report.

This may be useful as a conversation starter, but it is not yet an AI-ready task.

A better AI-ready task is more specific:

Review this report introduction for clarity, audience fit, and logical flow.
Do not rewrite the technical findings.
Return three sections:
1. strengths,
2. unclear areas,
3. suggested revision priorities.

The second version gives the AI a defined role, a scope, constraints, and an expected output format.

The human still decides what to accept.

The CDI Problem-Framing Pattern

A practical AI problem statement should answer seven questions.

1. What is the real-world problem?
2. Who is the intended user or audience?
3. What input will the AI receive?
4. What should the AI produce?
5. What should the AI not do?
6. How will the output be evaluated?
7. What human decision or action follows?

These questions keep AI work connected to purpose, context, and review.

They also make the task easier to reproduce later.

Example: Weak vs Strong Framing

Weak framing:

Use AI to analyze survey responses.

This is too broad.

It does not say what kind of analysis is needed, what the survey is about, what outputs are expected, or how the results will be used.

Stronger framing:

We have short-text survey responses from training participants.
The goal is to identify common themes about what learners found useful and what they found confusing.
The AI should group responses into themes, provide short labels, and include representative examples.
The AI should not infer demographic information or make claims beyond the provided responses.
A human reviewer will check the themes before they are included in the final report.

This version is clearer because it defines the purpose, input, output, limits, and review step.

Task Type Matters

Not all AI tasks are the same.

A task may involve:

  • summarization;
  • drafting;
  • classification;
  • extraction;
  • comparison;
  • brainstorming;
  • translation;
  • code assistance;
  • data interpretation;
  • workflow automation;
  • decision support.

Each task type requires different framing.

Summarization requires source boundaries.

Drafting requires audience and tone.

Extraction requires fields and rules.

Classification requires categories and examples.

Data interpretation requires assumptions and limits.

Decision support requires strong human review.

The task type determines the prompt structure, evaluation strategy, and risk controls.

AI Task Risk Levels

A useful CDI AI workflow separates low-risk tasks from higher-risk tasks.

Low risk
- brainstorming titles
- summarizing non-sensitive public text
- improving grammar
- generating study questions

Moderate risk
- summarizing internal notes
- drafting professional communication
- extracting information from documents
- preparing analytical interpretations

High risk
- medical, legal, financial, or safety-related advice
- decisions affecting people, eligibility, employment, or access
- automated judgments without human review
- use of sensitive or private data

Higher-risk tasks require stronger constraints, better documentation, and more human review.

In some cases, AI should support the workflow but not produce the final answer alone.

The Human Decision Context

Every AI task should be connected to a human decision context.

Ask:

What will someone do with the AI output?

Will they publish it?

Will they send it to someone?

Will they use it to make a decision?

Will they use it to prioritize work?

Will they use it to interpret data?

The more consequential the downstream action, the more careful the problem framing must be.

The AI output is not the endpoint.

The human action after the AI output is the endpoint.

A Reusable Problem-Framing Template

Use this template before building a prompt or workflow.

Project or workflow name:

Problem:
What real-world problem are we trying to address?

Audience or user:
Who will use the output?

AI task type:
Is this summarization, drafting, extraction, classification, analysis, automation, or decision support?

Input:
What information will be provided to the AI?

Expected output:
What should the AI return?

Boundaries:
What should the AI avoid doing?

Risk level:
Is this low, moderate, or high risk?

Evaluation:
How will we check whether the output is good enough?

Human review:
Who reviews the output before it is used?

Final human action:
What decision, communication, report, or workflow step follows?

This template can be saved as part of project documentation.

It can also be used before creating prompt libraries, automation scripts, or AI-assisted reports.

Problem Framing Before Prompting

Prompt design is easier after problem framing.

Without framing, prompt writing becomes guesswork.

With framing, the prompt can inherit the structure of the task.

For example, a framed task can be converted into a prompt like this:

Role:
You are helping review short-text survey responses from a training workshop.

Task:
Identify common themes about what learners found useful and what they found confusing.

Input:
The responses are provided below.

Output format:
Return a table with theme, description, example response, and reviewer note.

Boundaries:
Do not infer demographic information.
Do not make claims beyond the provided text.

Review expectation:
The output will be checked by a human before reporting.

The prompt becomes stronger because the problem was framed first.

Common Framing Mistakes

Common mistakes include:

Mistake 1: Asking AI to solve a problem before defining the problem.
Mistake 2: Giving AI a task without identifying the audience.
Mistake 3: Asking for analysis without explaining how the result will be used.
Mistake 4: Treating a high-risk decision as a simple writing task.
Mistake 5: Forgetting to define what the AI should not do.
Mistake 6: Skipping human review because the output sounds confident.

These mistakes are preventable.

The solution is not necessarily a more complex prompt.

The solution is a clearer problem frame.

Mini Case Study: Framing an AI Reporting Task

Suppose a team wants to use AI to help prepare a monthly project update.

A weak request might be:

Write a project update using these notes.

A stronger problem frame might be:

Problem:
The team needs a concise monthly project update for internal stakeholders.

Audience:
Project leads and collaborators who need progress, blockers, and next steps.

Input:
Meeting notes, task list, and recent completed activities.

Expected output:
A structured update with three sections: progress, blockers, and next steps.

Boundaries:
Do not invent progress that is not in the notes.
Do not assign blame.
Do not include private comments.

Risk level:
Moderate, because the update may influence stakeholder expectations.

Evaluation:
A human reviewer checks accuracy against the notes before sending.

Final action:
The reviewed update is sent to stakeholders.

This framing makes the AI task clearer, safer, and easier to evaluate.

Practical Exercise

Choose one AI task you currently do or want to do.

Before writing a prompt, complete the problem-framing template.

Then rewrite your original request as an AI-ready task.

Use this structure:

Original request:

Framed problem:

AI-ready task:

Risk level:

Required human review:

The goal is not to make the prompt longer.

The goal is to make the work clearer.

Chapter Summary

Problem framing is the first step in responsible AI systems work.

It defines the purpose, audience, input, output, boundaries, evaluation method, and human decision context.

A well-framed AI task is easier to prompt, easier to evaluate, and safer to use.

The Human–AI–Human workflow begins with the human responsibility to define the problem clearly.
