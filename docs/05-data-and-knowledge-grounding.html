Data and Knowledge Grounding

Published

Jun 2026

  • ID: AI-005
  • Type: Core Chapter
  • Audience: Learners, analysts, educators, researchers, and professionals building AI-supported workflows
  • Theme: AI outputs become more useful when they are grounded in appropriate data, source material, and documented knowledge

Chapter Purpose

AI systems are most useful when they are connected to the right context.

A prompt can ask a clear question.

But a clear question is not enough if the AI system does not have the information needed to answer well.

When source material, data, definitions, prior decisions, and constraints are missing, the AI may rely on general patterns instead of project-specific evidence.

That can produce fluent but unsupported output.

This chapter introduces data and knowledge grounding as a core AI systems skill.

Grounding means connecting AI work to the information that should shape the answer.

In a CDI workflow, grounding helps move AI use from casual assistance toward reproducible, reviewable, and defensible work.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • Explain what grounding means in an AI workflow.
  • Distinguish between general AI knowledge, user-provided context, project data, and authoritative sources.
  • Identify when an AI output is insufficiently grounded.
  • Design grounded prompts that separate source-based claims from interpretation.
  • Use simple project files to organize AI-ready knowledge.
  • Apply the Human–AI–Human workflow to source review, evidence use, and documentation.

What Grounding Means

Grounding means linking an AI task to relevant evidence or context.

In practical work, grounding may include:

  • a dataset,
  • a table of results,
  • a report,
  • a protocol,
  • meeting notes,
  • a style guide,
  • a project decision log,
  • a reference document,
  • a glossary,
  • or a trusted external source.

The purpose is simple.

The AI should not have to guess the project context.

The human supplies or identifies the information that matters.

Then the AI assists within that context.

The Human–AI–Human Grounding Loop

Grounding follows the Human–AI–Human workflow.

Human selects the relevant sources and context
        ↓
AI summarizes, extracts, compares, reorganizes, or drafts
        ↓
Human verifies claims against the sources and decides what to use

The human role is especially important at the beginning and the end.

At the beginning, the human decides what counts as relevant source material.

At the end, the human checks whether the AI output is faithful to that material.

AI can assist with evidence organization.

It should not silently replace evidence judgment.

Why Ungrounded AI Outputs Are Risky

AI-generated text can sound confident even when it is weakly supported.

This is one of the main reasons grounding matters.

An ungrounded AI output may:

  • invent details,
  • overgeneralize,
  • miss project-specific constraints,
  • mix facts with assumptions,
  • cite unclear evidence,
  • or produce recommendations that do not fit the actual situation.

In low-risk brainstorming, this may be acceptable.

In analysis, reporting, education, governance, health, finance, or organizational decision-making, it is not enough.

The stronger the consequence of the output, the stronger the grounding and review process should be.

Types of Grounding

Not all grounding is the same.

Different tasks require different forms of context.

Grounding type What it provides Example
Task grounding Defines the work “Extract action items from these meeting notes.”
Source grounding Limits claims to provided material “Use only the attached report.”
Data grounding Connects output to structured data “Interpret this results table.”
Domain grounding Adds subject-matter context “This is for a clinical data workflow.”
Decision grounding Connects work to prior choices “Use the approved inclusion criteria.”
Style grounding Aligns presentation “Write in CDI guide style.”
Governance grounding Adds rules and constraints “Do not include confidential information.”

A strong AI workflow makes the grounding type explicit.

General Knowledge Is Not Enough

Large AI models can provide useful general explanations.

But general knowledge is not the same as project knowledge.

For example, an AI tool may explain what evaluation means in machine learning.

That does not mean it knows:

  • which dataset your project uses,
  • which metric your team selected,
  • which tradeoffs were accepted,
  • which assumptions were documented,
  • which audience will read the report,
  • or which decisions depend on the output.

General AI knowledge can support understanding.

Project-specific knowledge must come from the project.

Source-Grounded Prompts

A source-grounded prompt gives the AI a clear boundary.

For example:

Task:
Summarize the source text below into key decisions, open questions, and next steps.

Source boundary:
Use only the source text provided. Do not add external facts.

Output format:
Return three sections: Decisions, Open Questions, Next Steps.

Review rule:
If a point is not clearly supported by the source text, place it under Open Questions instead of presenting it as a decision.

This prompt does not only ask for a summary.

It defines what evidence is allowed.

It also gives the AI a way to handle uncertainty.

Data-Grounded Prompts

Data-grounded prompts are especially important in analytical workflows.

The AI should know what the table represents, what columns mean, and what it should avoid claiming.

For example:

Task:
Review the following AI evaluation summary table and identify quality patterns.

Data context:
Each row represents an AI-assisted task. Columns include task_id, task_type, risk_level, output_quality_score, review_status, and reviewer_notes.

Constraints:
Do not make claims beyond the table. Do not treat a quality score as proof of correctness. Separate observations from recommendations.

Output format:
Return a table with columns: observation, supporting_field, possible_interpretation, recommended_human_review.

This helps keep the AI output connected to the actual data.

It also prevents the AI from presenting unsupported conclusions as facts.

Knowledge Grounding with Project Files

A practical AI system often needs a small knowledge structure.

This does not need to be complex at the beginning.

A simple project can include:

data/
├── example/
│   └── ai-task-log.csv
├── input/
│   └── project-context.md
├── results/
│   ├── ai-evaluation-summary.csv
│   └── prompt-registry.csv
└── reports/
    └── ai-evaluation-summary.png

The goal is to make context reusable.

Instead of rewriting the same background every time, the project can maintain stable files that describe the work.

Examples include:

  • a project overview,
  • a glossary,
  • a task registry,
  • a prompt registry,
  • an evaluation rubric,
  • and a decision log.

These files become the memory of the workflow.

Example Project Context File

A simple project context file can help ground AI work.

Project name:
CDI AI Systems Guide

Purpose:
Teach learners how to design responsible, reproducible, human-led AI workflows.

Core workflow:
Human defines the problem.
AI assists with generation, reasoning, analysis, or automation.
Human evaluates, decides, documents, and acts.

Audience:
Learners, analysts, educators, researchers, and professionals.

Quality expectations:
Outputs should be clear, reviewable, source-aware, and cautious about unsupported claims.

Boundaries:
Do not treat AI output as final evidence. Do not include confidential information in prompts. Document assumptions and review steps.

This type of file can be reused when drafting chapters, reviewing outputs, designing prompts, or building examples.

Grounded Outputs Should Separate Claims

A grounded AI output should make it clear what kind of statement is being made.

Useful categories include:

Statement type Meaning Review need
Source-based observation Directly supported by provided material Check against the source
Interpretation Human or AI explanation of what the observation may mean Review for logic and context
Assumption Something not directly proven by the source Confirm or revise
Recommendation Suggested next action Review for feasibility and risk
Uncertainty Something unclear or missing Investigate further

This structure prevents a common AI failure: blending evidence, inference, and recommendation into one confident paragraph.

Grounding and Citations

Grounding does not always require formal citations.

But it does require traceability.

Traceability means the human can answer questions such as:

  • What source was used?
  • What data table supported the claim?
  • What assumption was made?
  • What was generated by AI?
  • What was verified by a human?
  • What still needs review?

In public reports, academic writing, technical documentation, or high-stakes contexts, citations and source references become more important.

In internal workflows, a clear decision log or source note may be enough.

The principle is the same: important claims should be traceable.

Knowledge Grounding and Retrieval

Some AI systems use retrieval to bring relevant documents into the model context.

This is often called retrieval-augmented generation.

The idea is straightforward:

User asks a question
        ↓
System retrieves relevant source material
        ↓
AI generates an answer using that material
        ↓
Human reviews the answer and checks the sources

Retrieval can improve grounding, but it does not remove the need for review.

The retrieved material may be incomplete, outdated, irrelevant, or misinterpreted.

The system still needs human evaluation.

Common Grounding Mistakes

Several grounding mistakes appear often in AI workflows.

Mistake Why it matters Better practice
No source boundary AI may add unsupported material State what sources may be used
Too much context Important details may be buried Provide the most relevant context first
Missing definitions AI may interpret terms differently Include a glossary or definitions
Unclear data columns AI may misread the table Explain column meanings
No uncertainty handling Weak claims may appear final Ask AI to flag unclear points
No review trail Hard to audit later Keep prompts, outputs, and review notes

Grounding is not only about adding more information.

It is about adding the right information in a reviewable way.

Supporting Workflow

This guide includes example scripts that create and summarize an AI task log.

The task log acts as a simple grounding artifact because it records what AI was used for, what risk level was assigned, and whether review occurred.

Run from the project root:

bash scripts/bash/01-create-example-ai-task-log.sh

python scripts/python/02-evaluate-ai-output-quality.py \
  data/example/ai-task-log.csv \
  data/results

python scripts/python/03-build-prompt-registry.py \
  data/example/ai-task-log.csv \
  data/results

Expected outputs:

data/example/ai-task-log.csv
data/results/ai-evaluation-summary.csv
data/results/prompt-registry.csv

These outputs are not meant to be a complete AI governance system.

They are starter artifacts for documenting AI-assisted work.

Practical Checklist

Before using AI with source material or project data, ask:

  • What source or data should ground the output?
  • What information is allowed?
  • What information is missing?
  • What claims must be separated from assumptions?
  • What should the AI avoid adding?
  • How will the human verify the output?
  • Where will the prompt, source, output, and review decision be documented?

These questions make grounding part of the workflow rather than an afterthought.

Chapter Summary

Data and knowledge grounding connects AI work to the information that should shape the output.

Grounding helps reduce unsupported claims, improves reviewability, and makes AI-assisted workflows more reproducible.

In CDI AI Systems, grounding is part of responsible Human–AI–Human work.

The human selects the relevant sources.

AI assists with organization, extraction, summarization, drafting, or analysis.

The human verifies, documents, and decides what can be used.
